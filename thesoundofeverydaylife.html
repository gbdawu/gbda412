In this workshop, You will embark on an auditory walk paying attention to the history woven into the streets surrounding the Stratford School of Interaction. As you move along St. Patrick Street and nearby landmarks, you’ll hear stories that evoke everyday life in Stratford. This workshop invites you to listen closely and discover how the city’s past and present continue to interact in the spaces we inhabit today.

  • Your smartphone (iOS or Android)

mdi-walkWhat is a Soundwalk?

A soundwalk is an exploration of the environment through listening. Participants move through a space, focusing on sounds both natural and human-made. Learn more here.

This workshop will guide you through collecting and editing audio to give you ideas for your soundwalk recordings. By the end, you'll have hands-on experience with audio capture and editing techniques.


mdi-targetLearning Objectives

  • Identify and extract sounds from archival media.
  • Capture your own recordings of campus life.
  • Layer recordings with narration or text-to-speech.

mdi-toolsTools you'll use

  • Headphones or earbuds
  • Sounds of Stratford website, Stratford School YouTube channel, Stratford City YouTube Channel
  • Your smartphone (iOS or Android)
  • Clipchamp (clipchamp.com)

mdi-lightbulbStep 1: Start with a Theme

Objective: Refine or reimagine your theme of everyday life at Stratford School and its surroundings. Your theme will guide your sound collection, editing, and connection to course readings.
  1. Revisit your proposal from last week. Remind yourself of the theme(s) you first imagined for your soundwalk. You can also see what other people are planning by reading the proposals on the discussion forum on LEARN.

  2. Reflect on how your theme can connect with ideas from Weeks 1–4. For example:
    • In what ways can walking and listening create a story or narrative of daily life on and around campus?
    • How do everyday sounds around Stratford shape your sense of place and belonging?
    • What sounds are associated with different bodies and spaces near Stratford School?
    • Are there sounds or places that carry gendered, cultural, or social meanings in the context of Stratford School and its surroundings?
    • How do rhythms, routines, or repetitions of sound reveal patterns of time in Stratford’s everyday environment?

  3. Write down a refined theme statement (1-2 sentences). Keep it flexible — your theme can evolve as you gather new sounds and ideas. If you haven't submitted a proposal yet, use this step to start your initial theme proposal (consult guideline on LEARN).

mdi-microphoneStep 2: Collect Sound Recordings

Soundwalks can include many different types of recordings. For your project, you'll get to choose between using existing audio from archival media or creating your own recordings with your cellphone or field recorder or even text-to-speech tools.


For today's workshop, start by exploring one of the sound collection streams below. If you finish one before class ends, try another! If you only get to one, that's fine too — you can always explore the others at home.

mdi-cellphone Stream 2: Record Sounds With Your Cellphone or Field Recorder
Objective: Capture present-day sounds on campus to provide a current perspective. You can choose to work with:
  • Your cellphone recorder
  • A field recorder from Stratford School Media Services
  1. Choose an go to a location near Stratford School to record an audio based on the theme you selected in Step 1. Follow the steps below based on your recording device:

    • If you choose to record with your cellphone:
      • Use your phone’s recorder to capture at least two to three different sounds related to your theme. Keep each recording short (30–60 seconds).
      • Follow these tips for good recordings:
        • Hold your phone steady and close to the sound source.
        • Avoid covering the microphone.
        • Minimize background noise (wind, traffic, etc.).
        • Record multiple takes if needed.
        Example
        Record near the entrance door of Stratford School. Capture layers of everyday sound such as footsteps, conversations, and doors opening.

        How this example connects to readings from Weeks 1–4:
        • Pattern of time: Notice how rhythms of arrival and departure mark the passage of time on campus. Consider whether certain voices or activities repeat daily, creating recognizable temporal patterns.
        • Sense of place and belonging: Reflect on how overlapping sounds form a shared soundscape, shaping feelings of community or exclusion in this space.
      • Back in the classroom, transfer your recordings to your computer. (Options: email them to yourself, use AirDrop, Google Drive, or OneDrive)
      • Rename your files descriptively (e.g., sounds_from_campus_entrance_door).
      • If your recordings are not already in .mp3 or .wav format, convert them using CloudConvert. These format are compatible with a range of devices from cellphones to laptops.

    • If you choose to record with the Zoom H6 field recorder:
      • Since you’ll be borrowing the H6 field recorder from Media Services, please scan the QR code below to complete the Equipment Loan Agreement for the new school year. This step is required in order to access the equipment:
      • Go to Media Services on the first floor of campus to borrow one Zoom H6 field recorder kit. (The Media Services technician is aware of this workshop and will be ready to loan out the equipment during today’s class.)
      • Familiarize with the Zoom H6 field recorder:
      • Before going out to record:
        • Check that the batteries and SD card are inserted and that you have fresh batteries.
        • Choose the microphone capsule (X/Y or shotgun) based on your sound source (e.g., use the X/Y capsule for capturing the overall ambience of a busy hallway, or the shotgun capsule for focusing on a single speaker/source at a distance).
        • PressLR buttons to turn on the microphone.
        • Press Record ⏺ once to start recording (the red light will be steady).
        • Press Play ▶ to listen to your recording.Use ⏮ or ⏭ to skip to other recordings.
        • When finished, stop the recording and power off before removing the SD card.
      • Go out to capture two to three different sounds related to your theme. Keep each recording short (30–60 seconds).
        • Follow these tips for good recordings:
          • Hold your phone steady and close to the sound source.
          • Avoid covering the microphone.
          • Minimize background noise (wind, traffic, etc.).
          • Record multiple takes if needed.
        Example
        Use the shotgun microphone to focus on a single sound source, such as a street performer near Stratford’s City Hall. Capture the voice or instrument in detail while reducing background noise from passing cars or conversations.

        How this example connects to readings from Weeks 1–4:
        • Bodies and spaces: Reflect on how isolating one sound changes the narrative of the soundscape — what story emerges when we privilege an individual performer’s sound over the surrounding environment?
        • Narratives of daily life: Consider how this emphasis highlights cultural or social meanings connected to music and public space, and how listening practices shape collective experience.
      • Back in the classroom, transfer your files via the SD card reader or USB cable.
      • Rename your files descriptively (e.g., performer_near_city_hall).

mdi-account-voice Stream 3: Voice Annotations or Narrations
Objective: Create audio based on your own narration using the text-to-speech feature in Clipchamp. You can:
  • Develop creative texts (e.g., stories, poems, reflections), or
  • Use factual texts (e.g., historical notes, news excerpts)
  1. Choose a location or space at Stratford School that connects to your theme from Step 1.

  2. Write a short script (2–3 sentences) describing your experience, observations, or reflections on everyday life in that space.
    • Your script can be creative (e.g., a poem or story) or factual (e.g., historical information or accessibility details).
      • For factual or historical information, explore the Streets of Stratford website.
    • Try linking your script to themes from the readings (e.g., environment, place and belonging, bodies and spaces, gender and culture, etc.).
    3. Example
    Start by exploring St. Patrick Street near Stratford School. Research its history on the Streets of Stratford website. Create 2–3 short audio recordings (60–90 seconds each), combined with text-to-speech narration that highlights historical facts, everyday sounds (footsteps, traffic, conversations), and reflections to tell a story of everyday life, past and present.

    How this example connects to readings from Weeks 1–4:
    • Environmental change: Consider how the soundscape has evolved over time, from historical events to the current everyday sounds of the street.
    • Social and cultural meanings: Think about how certain sounds, spaces, or activities may carry historical, cultural, or gendered significance.

  3. Generate an audio narration using Clipchamp's Text-to-Speech:
    • Sign in to Clipchamp:
      💡 Tip: Clipchamp is a free, browser-based video and audio editor that works well for this activity. If you already know another editing tool (like Audition or Audacity), feel free to use that instead.
      • Go to https://app.clipchamp.com/login
      • Click "Sign in for Clipchamp for Work or School Accounts" and sign in with your Stratford School email

    • Create a new project:
      • Click Create Video Project and give your project a descriptive name.
      Tip: We’re using Clipchamp because it makes it easy to work with both audio and video. You can detach audio from video clips, add fades, and even use text-to-speech if you’d like to include narration. However, if you’re more comfortable with another editing tool (e.g., Adobe Audition, Audacity), feel free to use that instead.

    • Add your text-to-speech narration:
      • On the left panel, select Record & Create → Text to Speech
      • Type your script into the text box and experiment with different voices and settings

    • Optional: Add background music or ambient sound to layer with your narration
      • On the left panel, select Music
      • Drag the desired music to the timeline

    • Optional: Edit your music clip:
      • Trim the music: Use the Split tool (scissor icon above the timeline) to cut unwanted portions
      • Add fade effects: Select your audio clip, then in the right panel select Fade to add Fade In and Fade Out effects
        • Adjust the duration of fades as needed (typically 0.5-2 seconds)

    • Export your audio recording (voice plus music):
      • Click the Export button in the top right corner
      • Select More Options
      • Choose Audio-only
      • Name your file
      • Click export
      • Save your finished audio file to your computer
