GBDA 412 - Audio Cultures

Group Project 2: Sonic Signals and Futures (35% total)

Working in teams of 2, you will choose one of two streams below and create an original composition that explores sonic culture in innovative ways. Your project should engage with readings and topics from weeks 7–10.

Stream A – The Body as Instrument: Explore how personal bio-data can generate unique sonic experiences that reflect movement, gesture, and the physicality of the body. You will collect your own GPS data using Strava, an app and online platform for tracking physical activity. You may also use wearable devices (e.g., Apple Watch, Fitbit) to collect heart rate data. This data will then be sonified using an online sequencer to create experimental musical outcomes. Explore Strava

Stream B – Creative Music Machines: Explore how artificial intelligence tools can help you generate music. Using the online tool ElevenLabs, you will produce a music composition from elaborate text prompts. To create your composition, you will use other AI tools like ChatGPT to collaborate in an interactive, co-creative process to produce music. Explore ElevenLabs

Overall Learning Outcomes

By completing this project, students will:

  1. Create experimental sound compositions: Develop technical proficiency in collecting, manipulating, and composing sound using personal bio-data/bio-signals and AI tools to produce original artwork that explores new sonic possibilities.
  2. Collaborate effectively in creative contexts: Work productively with partners to synthesize individual ideas into cohesive artistic work, while providing and integrating constructive peer feedback throughout the creative process.
  3. Connect artistic practice to theoretical frameworks: Analyze how sound-based art can illuminate theoretical ideas about sound, embodiment, and technology.
  4. Reflect critically on creative learning: Articulate creative decisions, evaluate different artistic approaches, and analyze personal development throughout the project process using clear written and oral communication.

What You Need to Do

Stream A uses body-generated data (GPS → MIDI → sequencer); Stream B uses AI-assisted co-creation (prompts → generated audio → edits).   • Decide which concept to finalize — select one proposal or combine compatible elements into a single, cohesive idea
  • Plan the structure of your piece (intro / body / sections such as verse/chorus/bridge / outro) aiming for 2–4 minutes total
  • Create and refine the composition using appropriate tools for your stream

  1. Final audio file (MP3 or WAV) — 2 to 4 minutes in length, uploaded to the LEARN dropbox "Group Project 2 – Part 3: Final Artistic Composition".
  2. Group materials submitted in the same dropbox containing the required stream-specific materials (see below). Share only one folder/link per group.
  3. Artist statement (300-500 words) uploaded as PDF or Word file to the same folder.

Materials Requirements per Stream)

Stream A – Body as Instrument:

  1. CSV data: Submit the final GPS to MIDI file.
  2. Online Sequencer link: Provide the public link to your final composition on OnlineSequencer.net.

Stream B – Creative Music Machines:

  1. Prompt documentation: Submit the set of AI co-creation prompts or conversation transcripts you used (text files, screenshots, or exported logs). These should show the iterative prompting strategy you followed.

Technical Tips & Recommendations

  • Stream A — test your CSV formatting and experiment with different CSV→MIDI mapping options (pitch range, velocity mapping from speed/heartbeat, time scaling). Use Online Sequencer to layer additional instrumentation and refine arrangement.
  • Stream B — iterate prompts, keep records of variations, and consider post-processing AI output (EQ, layering, human input) to shape a cohesive composition. If using generated vocals, check clarity and intelligibility before finalizing.
  • Keep backups of all original data, MIDI files, prompts, and project versions — you will include these in your materials folder.

Artist Statement Requirements

300–500 words addressing:

  • Which concept you chose and why (reference your Part 1 proposal)
  • How your materials and technical process were used in the final composition (CSV→MIDI steps, sequencing choices, prompt strategies, AI interventions)
  • Connections to course readings and themes from Weeks 7–10 (cite at least 1–2 readings, e.g., Ortiz on biosignal art, McLean & Dean on algorithmic music, White on AI and creativity)
  • Key creative decisions and challenges encountered

Additional Notes on Composition

  • Aim for a clear beginning, development, and ending across 2–4 minutes.
  • Balance experimental gestures (data-driven textures, algorithmic quirks) with musical clarity so listeners can follow the piece.
  • Document any places where you intervened heavily (manual editing, added instruments, human performance) so assessors can see how you shaped raw data/AI output into art.

What You Need to Do

Each team's final composition will be exhibited for the class. Students will explore different projects in class, listen to the audio compositions, and interact with any visual or data elements provided (e.g., GPS routes, co-creation prompts). During the exhibition, you will provide constructive oral feedback to your peers. The full class period is dedicated to this activity.

Learning Objectives

  • Experience and critically engage with peer-created experimental music compositions
  • Develop skills in providing constructive oral feedback on creative and conceptual work
  • Practice attentive listening and observation through embodied or AI-generated music projects
  • Learn to articulate responses to artistic work in oral discussion

Exhibition Structure

The exhibition will take the form of a project fair. Half of the teams will set up at designated booths with their laptops or devices to showcase their compositions, along with any associated data or visual elements. During the first hour, the other half of the class will circulate among the booths, explore the projects, and take notes.

After the first hour, the teams will switch roles: those who were circulating will now take the booths to present their work, while the first group circulates to explore and observe new projects.

The fair will be followed by a brief classroom discussion to share reflections and highlight insights from the exhibition.

Exhibition Participation

  • Be present for the full 2 hours of class
  • Experience the different audio compositions created by your peers, including their supporting visual or data elements
  • Actively engage by listening attentively and taking notes for feedback

Feedback Requirements

After exploring each composition, provide constructive oral feedback to the presenting team, focusing on:

  • Specific observations about what worked well conceptually and sonically
  • Concrete suggestions for improving clarity, creativity, or integration of data, biofeedback, or AI elements
  • Questions about creative choices, compositional structure, or presentation
  • Connections to course themes and readings from weeks 7–10

Example Feedback Prompts

  • Which aspects of the composition did you find most engaging, surprising, or thought-provoking?
  • How did the use of body data (Stream A) or AI tools (Stream B) contribute to the creative outcome?
  • Were the audio and any supporting visual/data elements integrated cohesively?
  • How did the work make you think differently about sound, embodiment, or machine collaboration?
  • What specific adjustments could enhance clarity, creativity, or impact?
  • How effectively did the work connect to course themes from weeks 7–10?
